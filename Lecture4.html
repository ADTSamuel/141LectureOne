The physics of photon capture

The longer the rod, the more likely that an entering photon (at length $x=0$) will be absorbed before exiting (at length $x=L$).

What is the probability, $P$, that a rod captures each entering photon as a function of rod length?

The physics of photon capture

A photon that enters a slice of rod has a small probability of being absorbed.

This differential probability is proportional to both rhodopsin concentration, $C$, and an absorption cross-section, $\sigma$ and the differential thickness of the slice:
\[p=\sigma C dx\]

The physics of photon capture

Divide the distance from 0 to $x$ into $N$ slices.

The probability that a photon entering any prior slice is absorbed by that slice is $p=\sigma C x / N$.

The probability that a photon is not absorbed by each prior slice is $q=1-\frac{\sigma C x}{N}$.

These probabilities must be multiplied to achieve the total probability of not being absorbed by all $N$ prior slices before reaching $x$:
\[ \left( 1- \frac{\sigma C x}{N} \right)^N \]

The physics of photon capture

The probability that an entering photon is first absorbed between $x$ and $x+dx$ is: $dP = \left( 1- \frac{\sigma C x}{N} \right)^N \sigma C dx$
In the limit of large $N$, this differential probability is $dP = e^ {- \sigma C x} \sigma C dx$.

The probability that an entering photon is absorbed by a rod within its length $L$ is an integral of these differential probabilities: \[ P = \int_0^L dP = 1-e^{- \sigma C L} \]

How long should a rod cell be?


Number of rhodopsin in a rod increases linearly with length: $N=CAL$

Fraction of absorbed photons (signal amplitude) increases asymptotically to 1 with rod length:
\[ P = \int_0^L dP = 1-e^{- \sigma C L} \] Number of thermal isomerizations (a source of dark noise events) will increase linearly with length

Binomial statistics

Binomial distribution: frequency of $k$ successes for $n=10$, $p=0.16$, and $N=1000$.

We seek the frequency and fluctuations in isomerizations for a large number of rhodopsins

    

Flip a biased coin with a probability of a head $p$ and the probability of a tail $q=1-p$

The probability of a given sequence (e.g., 10010) is $pqqpq$

There are a total of $2^n$ possible sequences. The number of these with $k$ heads and $n-k$ tails is: \[ \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \] Thus, the probability of exactly $k$ heads in $n$ flips, i.e., the binomial distribution is: \[ P(k;n,p) = \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k} \] The binomial theorem proves that the binomial distribution is normalized: \[ \sum_{k=0}^n P(k;n,p) = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k} = (p+q)^n = 1^n = 1 \]

Expectation values of the binomial distribution

The expectation value of $k$ is its mean value: \[ \left< k \right> = \sum_{k=0}^n k P(k; n,p) = \sum_{k=0}^n k \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} p^k q^{n-k} \] To evaluate this, note that when $k=0$, the term is $0$, and that $k/k!=1/(k-1)!$ \[ \left< k \right> = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} p^k q^{n-k} \] Factor out $np$: \[ \left< k \right> = np \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{(n-1)!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} p^{k-1} q^{n-k} \]

Change variables by substituting $m=k-1$ and $s=n-1$ \[ \left< k \right> = np \sum_{m=0}^s \frac{s!}{m!(s-m)!} p^m q^{s-m} \]

Central Limit Theorem

The mean of a binomial distribution is the expectation value of its variable: \[ \left< k \right> = np \]
The variance of a binomial distribution: \[ \sigma^2 = \left< \left(k -\left< k \right> \right) ^2 \right> = \left< k^2 \right> - \left< k \right>^2 = npq \]

The binomial distribution converges to a Gaussian distribution at large $n$ \[ P(k;n,p) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi npq}} \exp\left[-\frac{(k-np)^2}{2npq}\right] \]

\[ P(k;\mu,\sigma) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{(k-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \]

Optimum length

Signal exponentially reaches an asymptote with increasing rod length. Noise increases as the square root of rod length. There is one rod length that maximizes the signal to noise ratio!

    

If a rhodopsin has a probability $p_{\mathrm{dark}}$ of being activated by temperature, the mean number of activations will be this probability multiplied by the total number of rhodopsins in the cell: \[ \mu_{\mathrm{dark}} = CLAp_{\mathrm{dark}}. \] Since dark events are also Poisson distributed, the standard deviation in the number of dark events $\sigma_{\mathrm{dark}} = \sqrt{\mu_{\mathrm{dark}}}$.

Assuming that the photon travels parallel to the axis of the rod cell, this mean number $\mu = \sigma CL$.

The signal-to-noise ratio of this rod cell when a flash of $n$ photons is delivered to it is: \[ SNR = \frac{np_{\mathrm{abs}}}{\sqrt{\mu_{\mathrm{dark}}}} = n\frac{1-e^{-\sigma CL}}{\sqrt{CLAp_{\mathrm{dark}}}}. \]
