    • Signal transduction from receptors to motors

        


  • A signaling cascade allows chemoreceptors to communicate with flagellar motors.

  • Negative feedback counteracts signaling to allow cells to adapt

  • When more attractant is bound, methyl groups are added to receptors by the enzyme CheR

  • When less attractant is bound, methyl groups are removed by the enzyme CheB

    • Signal transduction from receptors to motors

        

  • Chemoreceptors form complexes inside the cells with the kinase CheA and CheW
  • CheA phosphorylates CheY, a diffusible messenger
  • The phosphorylated form of CheY interacts with the flagellar motors to induce tumbles.
  • CheY is dephosphorylated by CheZ .
  • Binding of attractants to receptors decreases the rate of CheY phosphorylation $\rightarrow$ tumbling drops.
  • Adaptation involves changes in the methylation of chemoreceptors.
  • Methylation increases the rate of CheY phosphorylation
  • CheR and CheB, add and remove methyl groups
  • To adapt, methylation must rise to overcome the suppression of receptor activity caused by the attractant binding.
  • CheA enhances the demethylating activity of CheB by phosphorylating CheB

    • Adaptation by methylation

        

  • Consider an enzyme, $E$, which is sensitive to an external signal $l$, such as a ligand.
  • Each enzyme molecule is at equilibrium between two states: an active state, in which it catalyses a reaction, and an inactive state, in which it does not.
  • Modification of $E$ affects the probabilities of the active and inactive states, and can compensate for the effect of the ligand.
  • In general, $A(l)=\alpha (l) E + \alpha_m (l) E_m$ where:
         • $E_m$ is concentration of modified enzyme
         • $E$ is concentration of unmodified enzyme
         • $\alpha_m(l)$ probability of active modified state
         • $\alpha(l)$ probability of active unmodified state

    • The effect of activity-dependent negative feedback

  • Consider a system for which only the modified enzyme can be active
  • The enzyme $R$, which catalyses the modification reaction $E \rightarrow E_m$ works at saturation
  • The enzyme $B$, which catalyses the reverse-modification reaction $E_m \rightarrow E$ can only bind to active enzymes
  • The reverse modification rate is a simple function of the activity:
    $ \frac{dE_m}{dt}=V_{max}^R - V_{max}^B \frac{A}{A+K_B} $
  • The system activity is continuously compared to a reference steady-state value \[ A^{st} = K_b \frac{V_{max}^R}{V_{max}^B - V_{max}^R} \]
  • For $A < A^{st}$ the modification increases, leading to an increase in $A$
  • For $A > A^{st}$ the modification decreases, leading to a decrease in $A$
  • The system always returns to its steady-state value
