The Perfect Monitor

The perfect monitor is permeable to ligand molecules and estimates the concentration $c_0$ by counting the molecules in its volume.

Since the molecules diffuse independently, the number of molecules $N$ is Poisson distributed.

    

For the Poisson distribution, variance equals the mean: $\delta N^2= \left< N \right>$:

\[ \frac{\delta c^2}{c_0^2} = \frac{\delta N^2}{\left< N \right> ^2}=\frac{1}{\left< N \right> }=\frac{1}{c_0 V} \]

Diffusion can help!

In time $T$, $M \approx T/\tau_D$ independent measurements, ($\tau_D \approx a^2/D$ is turnover time)

This reduces uncertainty: \[ \frac{\delta c^2}{c_0^2} = \frac{1}{M \left< N \right> } = \frac{1}{(T/\tau_D)c_0V}\approx \frac{1}{Dac_0T} \]

One dimensional random walk

Each particle steps to the right or to the left once every $\tau$ seconds

Moving at velocity $\pm v_x$, distance traveled is $\delta = \pm v_x \tau$.

Particle position after the $n$th step differs from the $(n-1)$th step by $\pm \delta$: \[ x_i (n)=x_i (n-1) \pm \delta \]

One dimensional random walk

Standard deviations increase with the square root of time

    

Squared coordinate changes with time: \[ x_i^2 (n) = x_i^2 (n-1) \pm 2 \delta x_i(n-1) + \delta^2 \]
Mean squared coordinate grows with time:

$ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \left[ x_i^2(n-1) \pm 2 \delta x_i(n-1)+\delta^2 \right] $

$ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \left< x^2(n-1) \right> + \delta^2 $

The Diffusion Coefficient

  • Since $x_i(0)=0$ for all particles $i$, $\left< x^2(0)\right>=0$.

  • Thus, $\left< x^2(1)\right>=\delta^2$, $\left< x^2(2)\right>=2\delta^2$, $\left< x^2(3)\right>=3\delta^2$, ..., and $\left< x^2(n)\right>=n\delta^2$.

  • It follows that the mean-square displacement is proportional to $t$, the root-mean-square displacement is proportional to the square-root of $t$.

  • Note that $n=t/\tau$, so that: $ \left< x^2(t) \right> = (t/\tau) \delta^2 = (\delta^2/\tau)t $

  • We define a diffusion coefficient, $D=\delta^2/2\tau$, so: \[ \left< x^2 \right> = 2Dt \]

    • Diffusion follows binomial statistics

    For a 1D random walk, a particle steps to the right with probability $p$ and to the left with probability $q$.

    \[ P(k;n,p)=\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}p^{k}q^{n-k} \] Displacement in $n$ trials, $x(n)$, is the number of steps to the right minus the number to the left times step length, $\delta$: \[ x(n)=\left[ k-(n-k) \right] \delta = (2k-n)\delta \]

    Since we know the distribution of $k$, we know the distribution of $x$.
    The two distributions have the same shape.

    Diffusion follows binomial statistics

    Mean particle displacement is:

    $ \left< x(n) \right> = (2 \left< k \right> -n)\delta $

    where $\left< k \right> = np.$

        

    Mean-square displacement is

    $ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \left< \left[ ( 2k - n ) \delta \right] ^2 \right> $

    $ \left< x^2(n) \right> = (4\left< k^2 \right> - 4 \left< k \right> n + n^2) \delta^2 $

    where $\left< k^2 \right> = (np)^2 + npq$

    The Central Limit Theorem

    When $n$ and $np$ are both large, the binomial distribution, $P(k;n,p)$ becomes:

    $ P(k)dk=\frac{1}{(2\pi \sigma^2)^{1/2}}e^{-(k-\mu)^2/2\sigma^2}dk $

        

    Substitute:
         $x=(2k-n)\delta$,
         $dx=2\delta dk$
         $p=q=1/2$
         $t=n/\tau$
         $D=\delta^2/2 \tau$,


    $ P(x)dx=\frac{1}{(4 \pi D t)^{1/2}}e^{-x^2/4Dt}dx $

    $P(x)dx$: the Gaussian probability of finding a particle between $x$ and $x+dx$.

    Variance: $\sigma_x^2=2Dt$.

    Standard deviation: $\sigma_x=(2Dt)^{1/2}$.

    Einstein-Smoluchowski

    $D=k_BT/f_r$

        

    If $v=\frac{dx}{dt}$ is the velocity of a particle, the average is given by equipartition:

         $ m \left< v^2 \right> = k_B T $

    A particle moving with respect to the fluid at the speed $v$, experiences a viscous resistance equal to $-f_r v$ according to Stokesâ€™ formula and fluid mechanics.

    Langevin's Derivation of Einstein-Smoluchowski

    Multiply Newton's equation of motion by $x$ and rewrite:

         $ \frac{m}{2} \frac{d^2 x^2}{dt^2} - m\xi^2 = -\frac{f_r}{2} \frac{dx^2}{dt} + Xx $

    Take the ensemble average of the equation of motion for a large number of particles:

         $\left< m v^2 \right> = k_BT$

         $\left< Xx \right> = 0$

    Define $\frac{d\left< x^2\right> }{dt}=z$

          $\frac{m}{2} \frac{dz}{dt} + \frac{f_r}{2}z= k_BT$

     The general solution of the differential equation:      $z=\frac{2k_BT}{f_r}+C e^{-\frac{f_r}{m}t}$

    Langevin's Derivation, cont'd

    $z=\frac{2k_BT}{f_r}+C e^{-\frac{f_r}{m}t}$

    The second term has a small time constant (nanoseconds for small particles) and vanishes:

         $ \frac{d\left< x^2 \right> }{dt} = \frac{2k_BT}{f_r} $

    Hence, for some period of time $\tau$:

         $\left< x^2 \right> = 2\frac{k_BT}{f_r}\tau$

    The diffusion coefficient is thus:

    $D=\frac{k_BT}{f_r}$

    Fick's First Law

    At time $t$ there are $N(x)$ particles at position $x$, $N(x+\delta)$ particles at $x+\delta$.

    At time $t+\tau$, half of each set will have stepped left or right.

    The net number crossing to the right will be: $ -\frac{1}{2}\left[ N(x+\delta)-N(x) \right] $

    To obtain the net flux, divide by the area normal to the $x$ axis and by $\tau$:

    $ J_x=-\frac{1}{2}\left[ N(x+\delta)-N(x) \right] /A\tau $

    Fick's First Law

    $ J_x=-\frac{1}{2}\left[ N(x+\delta)-N(x) \right] /A\tau $

  • Multiply by $\delta^2/\delta^2$ and rearrange:

              $J_x=-\frac{\delta^2}{2\tau} \frac{1}{\delta}\left[ \frac{N(x+\delta)}{A\delta}- \frac{N(x)}{A\delta} \right] $

  • Define $D = \delta^2/2\tau$ to be the diffusion coefficient:

              $ J_x=-D\frac{1}{\delta} \left[ C(x+\delta) -C(x) \right] $

  • In the limit $\delta \rightarrow 0$,

    • $ J_x=-D\frac{\partial C}{\partial x} $

    Fick's First Law cont'd

    The flux due to a linear concentration gradient $(C_2-C_1)/b$.

    Net movement from right to left occurs because there are more particles at the right.

        

  • Fick's First Law states that net flux (at $x$ and $t$) is proportional to the slope of concentration (at $x$ and $t$).
    The constant of proportionality is $D$.

  • For uniform distribution, slope is 0
         $\partial C/\partial x = 0$ and $J_x=0$.

  • If the slope is constant, i.e., if $\partial C/\partial x$ is constant, $J_x$ is constant. This happens when $C$ is linear in $x$.

    • Fick's Second Law

  • Fluxes through the faces of a box.
  • The area of each face is $A$.
  • The faces are normal to the $x$ axis.
  • In time $\tau$, $J_x(x)A\tau$ particles enter from left
  • $J_x(x+\delta)A\tau$ particles leave from right.
  • The volume of the box is $A\delta$.

    •     




    The number of particles per unit volume in the box must increase at the rate
    \begin{multline} \frac{1}{\tau}\left[ C(t+\tau) - C(t) \right]=\\ = - \frac{1}{\tau}\left[ J_x(x+\delta) - J_x(x) \right]\frac{A\tau}{A\delta}\\ = - \frac{1}{\delta}\left[ J_x(x+\delta) - J_x(x)\right] \end{multline} Fick's Second Law is when $\tau \rightarrow 0$ and $\delta \rightarrow 0$
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial J_x}{\partial x} \end{align}

    The Diffusion Equation

    Fick's First Law in three dimensions We have $J_x=-D\partial C/\partial x$, $J_y=-D\partial C/\partial y$, and $J_z=-D\partial C/\partial z$. These are components of a flux vector: \begin{align} \mathbf{J}=-D\boldsymbol{\nabla}C \end{align}
    Fick's Second Law in three dimensions We have $\partial C/ \partial t = -\left( \partial J_x/\partial x + \partial J_y/\partial y + \partial J_z/\partial z\right)$: \begin{align} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t}= -\boldsymbol{\nabla}\cdot \mathbf{J} \end{align}
    The Diffusion Equation Combining produces the 3D diffusion equation: \begin{align} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t}= D\boldsymbol{\nabla}^2 C \end{align}

    The spherical adsorber

  • Spherical adsorber of radius $a$
  • Infinite medium containing particles at concentration $C_0$.
  • Dashed arrows are lines of flux.

    •     

    If the problem is spherically symmetric, the flux is radial, \begin{align*} J_r=-D\partial C/\partial r \end{align*} and \begin{align*} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 \frac{\partial C}{\partial r} \right) \end{align*}

    The concentration at $r=a$ is 0.
    The concentration at $r=\infty$ is $C_0$.

    With these boundary conditions, the diffusion equation has the solution: \begin{align*} C(r)=C_0\left( 1-\frac{a}{r} \right) \end{align*}

    The spherical adsorber

  • Spherical adsorber of radius $a$
  • Infinite medium containing particles at concentration $C_0$.
  • Dashed arrows are lines of flux.

    •     

    Net migration (flux) of molecules is radially inward: \begin{align*} J_r = -D C_0 \frac{a}{r^2} \end{align*}

    The particles are adsorbed by the sphere at a rate equal to the area, $4\pi a^2$ times the inward flux $-J_r(a)$: \begin{align*} I=4 \pi D a C_0 \end{align*}

    Adsorption rate, $I$, is the diffusion current.

    Probability of capture

  • Spherical shell source, radius $b$
  • Spherical adsorber, radius $a$
  • Spherical shell adsorber, radius $c$

    •     

    Suppose a particle is released near a spherical adsorber of radius $a$ at a point $r=b>a$?

  • What is the probability that the particle will be adsorbed at $r=a$ rather than wander away?

  • Particles released at $r=b$ move inward and are adsorbed at $r=a$ at rate $I_{in}$ or move outward and are adsorbed at $r=c$ at rate $I_{out}$.

  • Steady-state concentration rises from 0 at $r=a$ to $C_m$ at $r=b$ and falls back to 0 at $r=c$

    • Probability of capture, cont'd

  • Spherical shell source, radius $b$
  • Spherical adsorber, radius $a$
  • Spherical shell adsorber, radius $c$

    •     

    Steady-state diffusion equation

              $ \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 \frac{\partial C}{\partial r} \right) =0$

    Boundary conditions

              $C(r=a) = 0$
              $C(r=b) = C_m$
              $C(r=c) = 0$

    Separate solutions in separate compartments:

    if $a \leq r \leq b$:

         $ C(r) = \frac{C_m}{1-a/b}\left( 1-\frac{a}{r}\right) $           $ J_r(r) = \frac{-DC_m}{1-a/b}\frac{a}{r^2} $

    if $b \leq r \leq c$:

         $ C(r) = \frac{C_m}{c/b-1}\left( \frac{c}{r}-1\right) $           $ J_r(r) = \frac{DC_m}{c/b-1}\frac{c}{r^2} $

    Probability of capture, cont'd

  • Spherical shell source, radius $b$
  • Spherical adsorber, radius $a$
  • Spherical shell adsorber, radius $c$

    •     

    The diffusion current from the spherical shell source to the inner adsorber is
    \begin{align} I_{in} = 4 \pi DC_m \frac{a}{1-a/b} \end{align}
    The diffusion current from the spherical shell source to the outer adsorber is
    \begin{align} I_{out} = 4 \pi DC_m \frac{c}{c/b-1} \end{align}
    The probability that a particle released at $r=b$ will be adsorbed at $r=a$ is the ratio:
    \begin{align} \frac{I_{in}}{I_{in}+I_{out}} = \frac{a(c-b)}{b(c-a)} \end{align}
    In the limit $c\rightarrow \infty$, the probability of being adsorbed is $a/b$.

    Sedimentation of small particles

    The density of particles suspended in a fluid in a gravitational field is given by the Boltzmann distribution: \[ \rho (z) = \rho_0 e^{-mgz/k_B T} \] because $E=mgz$

        

    Each particle sediments at a velocity given by the balance between gravitational force ($\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{g}$) and viscous force ($\mathbf{F}=- f_r\mathbf{V}$).

    Einstein relation

  • Upward flux due to diffusion is given by Fick's First Law

    • \[ J_z^{diff} = D \times \frac{mg}{k_B T} \times \rho_0 e^{-\frac{mgz}{k_B T} }\]

  • Downward flux due to sedimentation is given by the product of velocity and particle density

    • \[ J_z^{sed} = - \frac{mg}{f_r} \times \rho_0 e^{-\frac{mgz}{k_B T} } \]

  • At steady state, $J_z^{diff}+J_z^{sed}=0$:

    • \[ D \times \frac{mg}{k_B T} \times \rho_0 e^{-\frac{mgz}{k_B T} } - \frac{mg}{f_r} \times \rho_0 e^{-\frac{mgz}{k_B T} }=0 \]

    Hence, $\frac{D}{k_B T} - \frac{1}{f_r} = 0$      and      $D=\frac{k_B T}{f_r}$
