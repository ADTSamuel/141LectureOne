The Perfect Monitor

The perfect monitor is permeable to ligand molecules and estimates the concentration $c_0$ by counting the molecules in its volume.

Since the molecules diffuse independently, the number of molecules $N$ is Poisson distributed.

    

For the Poisson distribution, variance equals the mean: $\delta N^2= \bar{N}$:

\[ \frac{\delta c^2}{c_0^2} = \frac{\delta N^2}{\bar{N}^2}=\frac{1}{\bar{N}}=\frac{1}{c_0 V} \]

Diffusion can help!

In time $T$, $M \approx T/\tau_D$ independent measurements, ($\tau_D \approx a^2/D$ is turnover time)

This reduces uncertainty: \[ \frac{\delta c^2}{c_0^2} = \frac{1}{M\bar{N}} = \frac{1}{(T/\tau_D)c_0V}\approx \frac{1}{Dac_0T} \]

One dimensional random walk

Each particle steps to the right or to the left once every $\tau$ seconds

Moving at velocity $\pm v_x$, distance traveled is $\delta = \pm v_x \tau$.

Particle position after the $n$th step differs from the $(n-1)$th step by $\pm \delta$: \[ x_i (n)=x_i (n-1) \pm \delta \]

One dimensional random walk

The mean displacement does not change: \[ \left< x(n) \right> = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \left[ x_i(n-1) \pm \delta \right] \] \[ \left< x(n) \right> = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i(n-1) \] \[ \left< x(n) \right> = \left< x(n-1) \right> \]

Standard deviations increase with the square root of time

    

Squared coordinate changes with time: \[ x_i^2 (n) = x_i^2 (n-1) \pm 2 \delta x_i(n-1) + \delta^2 \]
Mean squared coordinate grows with time:

$ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N x_i^2(n) $

$ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \left[ x_i^2(n-1) \pm 2 \delta x_i(n-1)+\delta^2 \right] $

$ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \left< x^2(n-1) \right> + \delta^2 $

The Diffusion Coefficient

  • Since $x_i(0)=0$ for all particles $i$, $\left< x^2(0)\right>=0$.

  • Thus, $\left< x^2(1)\right>=\delta^2$, $\left< x^2(2)\right>=2\delta^2$, $\left< x^2(3)\right>=3\delta^2$, ..., and $\left< x^2(n)\right>=n\delta^2$.

  • It follows that the mean-square displacement is proportional to $t$, the root-mean-square displacement is proportional to the square-root of $t$.

  • Note that $n=t/\tau$, so that: $ \left< x^2(t) \right> = (t/\tau) \delta^2 = (\delta^2/\tau)t $

  • We define a diffusion coefficient, $D=\delta^2/2\tau$, so: \[ \left< x^2 \right> = 2Dt \]

    • Diffusion follows binomial statistics

    For a 1D random walk, a particle steps to the right with probability $p$ and to the left with probability $q$.

    \[ P(k;n,p)=\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}p^{k}q^{n-k} \] Displacement in $n$ trials, $x(n)$, is the number of steps to the right minus the number to the left times step length, $\delta$: \[ x(n)=\left[ k-(n-k) \right] \delta = (2k-n)\delta \]

    Since we know the distribution of $k$, we know the distribution of $x$.
    The two distributions have the same shape.

    Diffusion follows binomial statistics

    Mean particle displacement is:

    $ \left< x(n) \right> = (2 \left< k \right> -n)\delta $

    where $\left< k \right> = np.$

        

    Mean-square displacement is

    $ \left< x^2(n) \right> = \left< \left[ ( 2k - n ) \delta \right] ^2 \right> $

    $ \left< x^2(n) \right> = (4\left< k^2 \right> - 4 \left< k \right> n + n^2) \delta^2 $

    where $\left< k^2 \right> = (np)^2 + npq$

    The Central Limit Theorem

    When $n$ and $np$ are both large, the binomial distribution, $P(k;n,p)$ becomes:

    $ P(k)dk=\frac{1}{(2\pi \sigma^2)^{1/2}}e^{-(k-\mu)^2/2\sigma^2}dk $

        

    Substitute:
         $x=(2k-n)\delta$,
         $dx=2\delta dk$
         $p=q=1/2$
         $t=n/\tau$
         $D=\delta^2/2 \tau$,


    $ P(x)dx=\frac{1}{(4 \pi D t)^{1/2}}e^{-x^2/4Dt}dx $

    $P(x)dx$: the Gaussian probability of finding a particle between $x$ and $x+dx$.

    Variance: $\sigma_x^2=2Dt$.

    Standard deviation: $\sigma_x=(2Dt)^{1/2}$.

    Einstein-Smoluchowski

    $D=k_BT/f_r$

        

    If $v=\frac{dx}{dt}$ is the velocity of a particle, the average is given by equipartition:

         $ m \left< v^2 \right> = k_B T $

    A particle moving with respect to the fluid at the speed $v$, experiences a viscous resistance equal to $-f_r v$ according to Stokes’ formula and fluid mechanics.

    Langevin's Derivation of Einstein-Smoluchowski

    Multiply Newton's equation of motion by $x$ and rewrite:

         $ \frac{m}{2} \frac{d^2 x^2}{dt^2} - m\xi^2 = -\frac{f_r}{2} \frac{dx^2}{dt} + Xx $

    Take the ensemble average of the equation of motion for a large number of particles:

         $\left< m v^2 \right> = k_BT$

         $\left< Xx \right> = 0$

    Define $\frac{d\left< x^2\right> }{dt}=z$

          $\frac{m}{2} \frac{dz}{dt} + \frac{f_r}{2}z= k_BT$

     The general solution of the differential equation:      $z=\frac{2k_BT}{f_r}+C e^{-\frac{f_r}{m}t}$

    Langevin's Derivation, cont'd

    $z=\frac{2k_BT}{f_r}+C e^{-\frac{f_r}{m}t}$

    The second term has a small time constant (nanoseconds for small particles) and vanishes:

         $ \frac{d\left< x^2 \right> }{dt} = \frac{2k_BT}{f_r} $

    Hence, for some period of time $\tau$:

         $\left< x^2 \right> = 2\frac{k_BT}{f_r}\tau$

    The diffusion coefficient is thus:

    $D=\frac{k_BT}{f_r}$

    Fick's First Law

    At time $t$ there are $N(x)$ particles at position $x$, $N(x+\delta)$ particles at $x+\delta$.

    At time $t+\tau$, half of each set will have stepped left or right.

    The net number crossing to the right will be: $ -\frac{1}{2}\left[ N(x+\delta)-N(x) \right] $

    To obtain the net flux, divide by the area normal to the $x$ axis and by $\tau$:

    $ J_x=-\frac{1}{2}\left[ N(x+\delta)-N(x) \right] /A\tau $

    Fick's First Law

    $ J_x=-\frac{1}{2}\left[ N(x+\delta)-N(x) \right] /A\tau $

  • Multiply by $\delta^2/\delta^2$ and rearrange:

              $J_x=-\frac{\delta^2}{2\tau} \frac{1}{\delta}\left[ \frac{N(x+\delta)}{A\delta}- \frac{N(x)}{A\delta} \right] $

  • Define $D = \delta^2/2\tau$ to be the diffusion coefficient:

              $ J_x=-D\frac{1}{\delta} \left[ C(x+\delta) -C(x) \right] $

  • In the limit $\delta \rightarrow 0$,

    • $ J_x=-D\frac{\partial C}{\partial x} $

    Fick's First Law cont'd

    The flux due to a linear concentration gradient $(C_2-C_1)/b$.

    Net movement from right to left occurs because there are more particles at the right.

        

  • Fick's First Law states that the net flux (at $x$ and $t$) is proportional to the slope of the concentration function (at $x$ and $t$).
    The constant of proportionality is $D$.

  • If the particles are uniformly distributed, the slope is 0
         $\partial C/\partial x = 0$ and $J_x=0$.

  • If the slope is constant, i.e., if $\partial C/\partial x$ is constant, $J_x$ is constant. This happens when $C$ is linear in $x$.

    • Fick's Second Law

  • Fluxes through the faces of a box.
  • The area of each face is $A$.
  • The faces are normal to the $x$ axis.
  • In time $\tau$, $J_x(x)A\tau$ particles enter from left
  • $J_x(x+\delta)A\tau$ particles leave from right.
  • The volume of the box is $A\delta$.

    •     




    The number of particles per unit volume in the box must increase at the rate
    \begin{multline} \frac{1}{\tau}\left[ C(t+\tau) - C(t) \right]=\\ = - \frac{1}{\tau}\left[ J_x(x+\delta) - J_x(x) \right]\frac{A\tau}{A\delta}\\ = - \frac{1}{\delta}\left[ J_x(x+\delta) - J_x(x)\right] \end{multline} When $\tau \rightarrow 0$ and $\delta \rightarrow 0$, we get Fick's Second Law
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial J_x}{\partial x} \end{align}
