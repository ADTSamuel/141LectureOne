The physics of sensory systems in biology

Aravi Samuel

Living organisms use their sensory systems to inform themselves of the sights, sounds, and smells of the environment.

All sensory systems are measuring devices that must operate within the limits of physical law.

Here, we consider the physics of sensory measurement, and study ways that biological systems have solved physical problems that lead to reliable sensory perception.

We will discuss specific cases in vision, olfaction, and hearing from the point of view of physics and information theory.

Hopfield algorithm

Start with $N$ neurons

Each neuron $i$ has two states: $V_i=0$ for `off' and $V_i=1$ for 'on'

$T_{ij}$ is the strength of the synaptic connection from neuron $j$ to neuron $i$.

$T_{ij}$ is positive/negative for excitatory/inhibitory connections, and zero for unconnected neurons.

Each neuron $i$ readjusts its state randomly in time (mean rate $W$).

     $V_i \rightarrow 1$ if $\sum_{j \neq i} T_{ij}V_j > U_i$

     $V_i \rightarrow 0$ if $\sum_{j \neq i} T_{ij}V_j < U_i$

Each neuron has a fixed threshold $U_i$ for switching (typically $U_i = 0$).
