The physics of sensory systems in biology

Aravi Samuel

Living organisms use sensory systems to inform themselves of the sights, sounds, and smells of their surrounding environments. Sensory systems are physical measuring devices, and are therefore subject to certain limits imposed by physics. Here we will consider the physics of sensory measurement and perception, and study ways that biological systems have solved their underlying physical problems. We will discuss specific cases in vision, olfaction, and hearing from a physicist’s point of view.

    • Thermal energy is everywhere

  • Classically, each degree of freedom of a system in thermal equilibrium has an average thermal energy:
         $E_{therm}=k_{B}T/2$

  • Three quadratic degrees of freedom:
         $mv_x^2/2 = kT/2$
         $mv_y^2/2 = kT/2$
         $mv_z^2/2 = kT/2$

  • Mean velocity:
         $v_{{{\text{rms}}}}={\sqrt {\langle v^{2}\rangle }}={\sqrt {{\frac {3k_{B}T}{m}}}}$

  • At room temperature:
         $k_{B}T \approx 4 \times 10^{-21} \text {J}$

  • The energy of one blue photon:
         $\sim 60 k_{B}T$

    • Thermal energy is statistically distributed


  • Thermal energy is exponentially distributed by the Boltzmann equation:
         $P(E)\propto e^{-{\frac {E}{k_{B}T}}}$

    • Bacterial chemotaxis

  • Bacteria count molecules in their environment
  • Chemoreceptor activity triggers a sensory transduction cascade that changes the concentration of molecules that affect flagellar rotation
  • CCW flagellar rotation corresponds to runs and CW flagellar rotation corresponds to tumbles
  • When the bacteria thinks life is getting better, runs get longer, leading to a biased random walk
